Catching drops with fibers

  TBC

The passive filtration of liquid droplets from aerosol streams or emulsions is done by placing a porous (generally fibrous) structure in the path of the mist; in particular, woven nets are used to harvest fresh water from fog, and non-woven entangled fibers are used to filter oil drops from gas streams. In this talk, I will present recent results on the capture of aerosol droplets with an array of parallel fibers. We identify the key role played by the drop distribution on the fibers on the overall collection efficiency. Due to a growth and coalescence process, this drop distribution evolves toward a regular pattern of uniformly distributed drops, and a balance between capillarity and gravity sets an average drop size. Accounting for these effects in a simple inertial impaction model allows a quantitative prediction of the collection efficiency. As the droplets accumulate on the fibers, they might merge and bridge adjacent fibers. If the fibers are flexible, they may deform under the capillary forces generated by these liquide bridges. In particular, we report and characterize a regime of strong elasto-capillary adhesion where the fibers are collapsed and form long liquid columns, efficiently capturing the incoming droplets.

Teng Zhang

Lecturer, Center for Automotive Engineering, Department of Mechanical Engineering Sciences

  University of Surrey

Dr Teng Zhang is a lecturer in Automotive Engineering in the department of Mechanical Engineering Sciences. He obtained his PhD from National University of Singapore in 2014 on multi-scale modelling of organic solar cells. Before joining University of Surrey, he spent 3 years as a research associate at Imperial College London working on modelling and testing of high energy-density lithium-sulfur batteries for automotive applications. Mathematical modelling of lithium-sulfur batteries

  TBC

The market demand for electric vehicles with longer driving ranges has heightened the need for high-energy-density batteries. Lithium-sulfur (Li-S) batteries could bring a step-change in the performance of automotive batteries with their 2-3 times higher energy density than today’s lithium-ion batteries. While already commercially viable in niche applications, wider market uptake of Li-S technology is currently hindered by a lack of understanding of their unique operational behaviours governed by complex the Li-S chemistry. In this talk, I will give a brief overview of the requirements and challenges of modelling Li-S batteries for informing battery design and control. Following the Occam's razor principle, a relatively simple Li-S model is derived that captures only those mechanisms that are required to reproduce the observable charge/discharge characteristics of Li-S batteries. I will show how such a model have delivered essential knowledge for commercially-sized lithium-sulphur batteries, such as identifying the probable main causes of discharge rate limits [1], charge rate limits [2], and decoupling their memory effect from irreversible degradation [3], despite being the most mathematically and computationally simple Li-S model in the literature. The potential application of the model for real-time Li-S battery control and management will be discussed. [1] T. Zhang et al., Electrochimica acta, 219, 502-508 (2016) [2] T. Zhang et al., J Electrochem. Soc., 165, 6001-6004 (2018) [3] M. Marinescu et al., J. Electrochem. Soc, 165, 6107-6118 (2018)

Bangti Jin

Reader in Inverse Problems, Computer Science

  University College London

Dr. Bangti Jin received Ph.D. degree in applied mathematics from the Chinese University of Hong Kong, Hong Kong, in 2008. Previously, he was an assistant Professor of mathematics at University of California, Riverside (2013-2014), a visiting assistant professor at Texas A&M University (2010-2013), an Alexandre von Humboldt Postdoctoral Researcher at the University of Bremen (2009-2010). He is currently a Reader in inverse problems in the Department of Computer Science, University College London, London, U.K. His research interests include computational inverse problems and numerical analysis of differential equations.

Stochastic gradient descent for inverse problems

  TBC

In this talk, I will discuss the stochastic gradient descent (including randomized Kaczmarz method) for solving inverse problems. Such algorithms have been very popular in practice, especially in computed tomography, and recently in machine learning. However, their theory for ill-posed inverse problems remains largely missing. In this talk, I will present some recent results in inverse problems, and some relevant results in machine learning.
